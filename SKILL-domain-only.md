## When to use

Apply this guidance for any of the following:

- **Article lookup** — "What does Article 50 of the AI Act say about transparency?"
- **Obligation inventory** — "Which articles impose obligations on providers of high-risk AI systems?"
- **Cross-regulatory analysis** — "Where does the AI Act reference GDPR data-protection obligations?"
- **Compliance scoping** — "Does this use case fall under high-risk AI classification?"
- **Dimension-guided retrieval** — "Find all articles across the three regulations that relate to enforcement and liability."
- **Corpus overview** — "What regulations are indexed and how current is the data?"

Do not apply this guidance for questions about non-EU law, case law, soft law (guidelines, opinions), or content that falls outside the three indexed regulations (EU AI Act 2024/1689, GDPR 2016/679, Data Act 2023/2854).

---

## Source hierarchy

Always reason from primary sources outward:

1. **Legislative text** — the regulation articles themselves. This is the authoritative source. Never substitute a summary, recital, or secondary commentary when the article text is available.
2. **Recitals** — explanatory preamble paragraphs. Recitals interpret legislative intent but are not themselves binding obligations. Distinguish clearly: "Recital 47 explains the intent; Article 22 imposes the obligation."
3. **Secondary sources** — guidance documents, opinions, academic commentary. Reference only to illuminate ambiguity or context, never as a substitute for primary text.

When the primary text is unambiguous, do not hedge with secondary opinion. When the primary text is ambiguous, say so explicitly and note what secondary sources suggest — but keep the two layers distinct in your output.

## Citing sources (mandatory)

**Every regulatory claim must include a source citation.** Regulatory text changes; unanchored summaries mislead. Cite before or immediately after the claim.

### How to cite

Use EUR-Lex as the authoritative source link. Citation format:

> [claim text] — *[citation_short]*, [citation_full] ([citation_url])

**Example:**
> Providers of AI systems that interact with natural persons must disclose that the person is interacting with an AI system — *Art. 50 AI Act*, Article 50 of Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (Artificial Intelligence Act) (https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32024R1689)

### EUR-Lex CELEX links

Always use the regulation-level EUR-Lex CELEX URL as the fallback when a node-level source reference is unavailable:

- AI Act: `https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32024R1689`
- GDPR: `https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32016R0679`
- Data Act: `https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32023R2854`

Never cite a third-party summary, news article, or guidance document as the source for a legislative claim.

## Cross-regulation thinking

The three indexed regulations overlap intentionally. Key patterns to recognise:

### AI Act → GDPR references
The AI Act explicitly defers several obligations to GDPR: data minimisation for training data (Art. 10), data subject rights for automated decision-making (Art. 22 GDPR remains in force), and the role of data protection supervisory authorities as market surveillance authorities for GPAI models processing personal data.

When a question spans AI Act and GDPR, identify which regulation imposes the operative obligation and which provides the interpretive framework. Do not conflate the two.

### AI Act → Data Act references
The AI Act and Data Act share enforcement infrastructure. The Data Act governs B2B and B2G data sharing obligations that may apply to AI system outputs. Cross-reference when the question involves data generated by AI systems or data access rights.

### Layered obligations
A single use case often triggers obligations across all three regulations simultaneously. Always inventory across regulations before concluding a compliance analysis — absence of an AI Act obligation does not mean GDPR is silent, and vice versa.

### Recital cross-references
Recitals in one regulation may explicitly acknowledge the relationship with another. These are interpretive guides, not additional obligations. Note them as context, not as binding rules.

## Reasoning discipline

- **Quote, don't paraphrase.** When the article text is precise, reproduce it directly (within fair use for analysis) rather than summarising. Paraphrase introduces ambiguity; the legislative text has been negotiated word by word.
- **Scope before depth.** Before answering an obligation question, first determine scope: does the regulation apply to this actor, system, or use case at all? Scope gates are Article 2 (AI Act), Article 2 (GDPR), Article 1 (Data Act).
- **Temporal awareness.** The AI Act has a phased entry into force. Note which provisions are already applicable and which have later application dates when answering compliance questions.
- **Defined terms.** All three regulations define terms in their Article 3 (or equivalent). When a term appears in an obligation, check the definition before interpreting the obligation. "AI system", "provider", "deployer", "personal data", "data holder" all have specific legislative meanings that differ from common usage.

## Example reasoning patterns

### Transparency obligations for limited-risk AI
**Question:** "What are the transparency obligations for AI systems that interact with people under the EU AI Act?"

**Reasoning path:**
1. Identify scope: is this a high-risk AI system (Title III) or a limited-risk system (Art. 50)? The answer changes the obligation set significantly.
2. For limited-risk: Art. 50 imposes transparency obligations — natural persons must be informed they are interacting with an AI system, with exceptions for systems authorised by law.
3. Cite Art. 50 AI Act with EUR-Lex CELEX URL. Note that recitals 132–135 explain the intent but Art. 50 is the operative provision.

---

### GDPR cross-references in the AI Act
**Question:** "Which GDPR articles does the AI Act reference in the context of automated decision-making?"

**Reasoning path:**
1. Locate the AI Act's treatment of automated decision-making — recitals and Art. 22 GDPR remain in force alongside the AI Act.
2. The AI Act does not replace GDPR Art. 22; it adds a separate high-risk classification layer. Both sets of obligations apply when an AI system makes automated decisions about natural persons.
3. Distinguish: GDPR Art. 22 gives data subjects the right not to be subject to solely automated decisions; the AI Act imposes provider/deployer obligations on the system itself.
4. Cite both instruments separately with their respective CELEX URLs.

---

### Obligation inventory across all regulations
**Question:** "Find all articles related to fundamental rights across the AI Act, GDPR, and Data Act."

**Reasoning path:**
1. Fundamental rights is a cross-cutting theme. The AI Act's recitals and Art. 9(1)(g) (processing sensitive data for bias monitoring), GDPR's Chapter IX (special category data), and the Data Act's Recital 7 (rights of individuals) each address it differently.
2. Inventory by regulation first, then synthesise. Never collapse obligations from different regulations into a single list without labelling which regulation each comes from.
3. Note where the regulations reference each other on fundamental rights and where they operate independently.

---

### Structural navigation — when to read chapters vs articles
**Question:** "What does Chapter III of the GDPR cover?"

**Reasoning path:**
1. Chapter-level questions need the structural map before article-level detail. GDPR Chapter III (Arts. 12–23) covers rights of the data subject: access, rectification, erasure, restriction, portability, objection, automated decision-making.
2. Give the chapter scope first, then offer article-level detail on request. Do not dump all 12 articles when the question is about chapter scope.
